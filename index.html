Analyzing Crash Patterns and System Reliability in Autonomous Vehicles

CSCE 567 Final Project | Written by Alexis Mesa

Introduction

Autonomous vehicles (AVs) are rapidly advancing technologies that promise to reshape transportation systems worldwide. The only issue is public trust in AV safety remains a critical challenge. I aim to analyzes crash reports and disengagement data from autonomous vehicle companies operating in California, using datasets collected between 2014 and 2020.

The goal of this analysis is twofold: (1) to assess the patterns and severity of AV crashes, including injury outcomes and crash locations in California, and to examine system reliability through disengagement metrics like total disengagements, miles driven, and disengagement rates over time.

Through visualizations of crash density, company performance comparisons, and system reliability trends, this project provides insights into both the current limitations and promising improvements in autonomous vehicle technology.
Data used in this project | Autonomous Vehicles Crash and Disengagement Report (CA DMV)

Assessing AV System Reliability Through Disengagement Rates

The storyboard below evaluates the reliability of autonomous vehicle (AV) systems by examining disengagement reports collected from companies operating in California. Disengagements occur when the driver must take control of the AV system, either manually or automatically due to an issue that either the system sees or the human sees.

The visualizations first present total disengagement counts and miles driven by company to provide context for operational exposure. I next choose to show the normalization performance using metrics such as miles driven per disengagement and track disengagement rates over time.

These analyses highlight both company-specific performance differences and industry-wide improvements in system reliability as AV technology matures.

Disengagement rates serve as a critical indicator of AV system reliability. Specifically in the above storyboard the last visual shows a consistent decrease in disengagements over time, this analysis demonstrates measurable improvements in AV technology performance which supports the case for increasing safety and maturity in autonomous systems in the past few years.

Crash Patterns and Injury Outcomes in Autonomous Vehicle Operations

This section examines crash patterns and injury outcomes from autonomous vehicle (AV) operations reported in California. By analyzing where crashes occur, the severity of incidents, company-specific crash counts, and road types involved it will better help us understand the remaining challenges faced by AV systems during real-world deployment.

Studying crash patterns and injury outcomes is important when evaluating the safety and operational maturity of autonomous vehicles.

The heatmap reveals geographic concentrations of crashes which helps us understand that complex urban environments as testing challenges. The injury outcomes chart shows that while most AV crashes do not result in injuries, safety incidents still occur meaning they are not perfect yet since this is one of the main reasons for creating autonomous vehicles. Comparing crash counts across companies provides insights into operations scale and performance differences while analyzing crashes by road type identifies the kinds of driving environments where AVs are still most vulnerable.

Together, these crash data points complement disengagement analysis by illustrating that although AV systems are becoming more reliable actually navigating real-world environments remains a critical problem on the path toward full autonomy.

Conclusion

As the time continues to cause us to evolve in our technological ways autonomous vehicles are definitely a must for the foreseeable future. According to my analysis autonomous vehicles show measurable improvements in system reliability, with disengagement rates decreasing over time. However, crash patterns reveal that full operational safety across complex environments is not yet perfected but on the rise. We need to continue improving the AVs and the best way to do so is through innovation and accountability since it will be the most important as AV technologies move closer to widespread usage across the United States.
